## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: The Unexpected Failure Mode

Starship Flight 13 aborted at T-0 to T+1 second on July 16, 2026, when 4 of 33 Raptor V3 engines on the Super Heavy booster failed to ignite during ground-level engine startup sequence. This represents a **novel failure mode** distinct from Flight 12's in-flight cascade failure and reshapes the risk calculus for SpaceX's orbital compute architecture decision.

### What Happened
- **Abort Trigger:** Engine ignition system failure (pre-launch ground test)
- **Engine Count:** 4 of 33 Raptor V3s failed to start
- **Timing:** T-0 to T+1 second (automatic abort protection activated)
- **Location:** Starbase, Boca Chica, Texas
- **SpaceX Response:** Replace 2 Raptor engines; retarget launch for early next week
- **Stock Impact:** -3% aftermarket trading; closes below IPO price for first time

### Critical Distinction from Flight 12 (May 22, 2026)

| Dimension | Flight 12 | Flight 13 |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Failure Stage** | In-flight boostback sequence (T+2:40-2:45) | Ground-level engine startup (T-0/T+1) |
| **Mechanism** | Engine relight failure during powered flight | Ignition system failure before liftoff |
| **Vehicle Loss** | Complete (hard splashdown, total loss) | None (automatic abort; safe recovery) |
| **Root Cause Clarity** | Ambiguous (FAA investigation required) | Immediate (2 engines require replacement) |
| **Organizational Signal** | Systemic architecture concern | Component-level engineering issue |
| **Market Impact** | -12.5% over 7 days | -2.3% same-day (modest decline) |

This bifurcation of failure modes—one in-flight and architectural, one ground-level and component-based—indicates SpaceX faces **layered engineering challenges** rather than a single root cause.
